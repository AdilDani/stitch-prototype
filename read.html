The Architecture of Silence

Cover plate — Citadel, eastern approach

The Citadel was quieter than it had any right to be. Iris had grown up listening to the low, constant hum of the city's infrastructure — the pumps, the vents, the distant drone of the trans-sector rail — but here, four thousand meters above the grid, there was nothing. Not even wind.

She walked the outer platform with the soles of her work boots rolling against the etched steel, each step producing a sound that seemed to exist only in her own skull. Emerine had warned her about this. "They build the upper floors to absorb. Absorb sound, absorb light, absorb you if you're not careful."

She stopped at the rail. The city below was a quilt of lights, seamed and re-seamed, patched where the old infrastructure had refused to speak to the new. She could see the seams from here. She could always see the seams. It was, in its own strange way, the reason she had been hired.

"Every stitched thing was stitched by someone. The question you should be asking is not whether they are still here, but whether they are still listening."

The door behind her opened without a sound. She turned slowly. She had learned, years ago, never to flinch. Architect-0 stood in the frame, hands folded, eyes the color of wet concrete.

"You came alone," he said. It was not a question.

"You asked me to."

He studied her for a long moment. She waited. This was how conversations went up here, she had been told — they happened mostly in the pauses. She counted the beats. She listened to the absence of wind. She wondered, as she often did, whether the silence was designed or simply found.

· · ·

"The anomaly," he said at last, "has been growing. We have known this for three cycles. What we did not know, until very recently, is that it is growing toward you."

She almost laughed. She would have laughed, if the word toward hadn't hit her in the chest like a dropped wrench. She opened her mouth to say something clever, something dismissive, and found that the Citadel had stolen her voice along with everything else.

"How long," she said finally, and the question came out in a register she did not recognize, "have you known?"

Architect-0 did not answer. He turned, instead, and walked back into the dark of the corridor, and after a moment — the longest moment of her life — Iris Kain followed.
